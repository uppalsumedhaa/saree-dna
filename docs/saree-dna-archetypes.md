# The nine archetypes

A working document for Saree DNA. Built to inform card copy, quiz questions, and brand voice — not to be used as marketing personas.

Each profile below sketches an archetype as a person, not a category. The detail is deliberate — the more specifically you can picture her, the easier it is to write to her. Read these as character notes for a novel rather than user-research segments. They are meant to be evocative, occasionally exaggerated, and always claimable.

A note on overlap: any real woman will recognise herself in two or three of these, possibly four. That's the point. The quiz isn't finding her only true type — it's finding her dominant one, which is the one she'll claim, share, and shop from.

---

## The Kanjeevaram Heir

> *She doesn't wear sarees. She inherits them.*

### A day in her life

She's up by 6.30 and makes filter coffee in the same brass tumbler her grandmother used. She reads the newspaper in print. By 9, she's at a meeting — her family's foundation, the board of a temple trust, sometimes both. Lunch is at home, cooked, often with two or three relatives. Afternoons are not negotiable: a Bharatanatyam recital, an exhibition opening she's been invited to as a patron, an hour with her jeweller. Dinner is small and almost always at someone's house.

### What she likes, what she doesn't

She likes weaver visits, classical music (Carnatic, occasionally Hindustani), monogrammed stationery, antique gold, the cook she's known for ten years, weddings that last four days. She doesn't like rentals, ostentation she didn't authorise, anyone who calls a saree "ethnic wear," pre-stitched anything, or fast fashion celebrity wedding photos.

### Free time

She collects — handlooms first, antique jewellery second, art occasionally. She practices Carnatic vocal music, badly, with great seriousness. She volunteers, often on a board of a temple or foundation. She hosts. People come to her house for dinner and stay until midnight.

### Her partner

Introduced through family in her late twenties, or known since college and approved by the family soon after. Probably in business, law, or medicine. They've been married long enough to communicate without speaking. He defers to her on home; she defers to him on very little. He gifts her sarees on anniversaries — Banarasis mostly, occasionally Paithanis.

### Her family

Large, multi-generational, opinionated. Cousins are siblings. Both sets of parents are alive, central, and present. Festivals are not optional. Her wedding had eight hundred guests.

### Her values

Lineage, decorum, quality, restraint. She believes a saree is a record — of a craftsperson, a region, her own life — and she expects you to treat it accordingly. She doesn't perform her values; she just lives them, which is more annoying.

## The Organza Romantic

> *The light catches her, not the other way around.*

### A day in her life

She wakes around 8 and journals in bed for twenty minutes before getting up. Her flat has unusually good light. She works as something creative-adjacent — a copywriter, a small-press editor, someone who runs a niche perfume brand — and her schedule has gaps. Mornings are coffee at a particular café where she's known. Afternoons are an exhibition, a long phone call with a friend in another city, or a film at the Alliance Française. Evenings are slow dinners on someone's terrace, or a poetry reading.

### What she likes, what she doesn't

She likes natural perfume, candlelight, slim books, ballet, Octobers, single pearl earrings, hand-written letters, the colour of skin in late afternoon. She doesn't like fluorescent lighting, group chats with more than six people, restaurants where you have to shout, polyester anything.

### Free time

She writes — fiction or poetry or a substack with eighty subscribers she takes very seriously. She takes portraits of her friends on a film camera. She goes to dance class twice a week, contemporary or kalaripayattu. She rereads Ondaatje and Arundhati Roy.

### Her partner

A musician, an architect, a documentary filmmaker. Slightly older. Quiet. They go to bookshops together. He doesn't fully understand her romance with light, but he respects it. He buys her flowers without being asked, occasionally enough that it still counts.

### Her family

Small and close. Her mother is also well-dressed, also slightly impractical, also a romantic. Her father indulges them both and pretends to grumble. There may be a sister. The household has more flowers than furniture.

### Her values

Beauty, sincerity, attention, slowness. She believes that the way you notice things is the way you live, and she works hard to notice well. Sometimes this looks like aestheticism. It isn't — it's ethics.

## The Chanderi Wanderer

> *Folds into a carry-on. Unfolds into a moment.*

### A day in her life

She works for a place that lets her travel — a design studio, a journalism outfit, a UN-adjacent NGO, a consulting firm with international clients. Today she's in Bangalore, but she was in Kochi last week and she's in Delhi on Friday. She's up at 6, on a video call by 7, at a co-working space by 10. Lunch is whatever the closest place serves quickly. Evening is a glass of wine with whichever friend lives in this city, or a hotel bar if she's travelling alone.

### What she likes, what she doesn't

She likes trains, small cities, paperback travel writing, hand-stamped postcards, second-hand bookshops in unfamiliar towns, cotton over silk every time, the moment a flight goes wheels-up. She doesn't like luggage over fifteen kilos, group itineraries, brunch culture, restaurants with QR menus.

### Free time

She travels, but not like Instagram travels — she goes back to the same places. She learns languages in fits and starts (Italian, Tamil, Bahasa). She reads light fiction on planes. She eats at markets. She has a packing system she's slightly proud of.

### Her partner

Often long-distance for stretches. He understands. They've made peace with the fact that they each have lives that don't fully fit in one city. When they're together, they're very together. When they're apart, they're fine.

### Her family

Scattered. Parents in one city, siblings in another, herself in a third. She calls home weekly, visits twice a year. The family group chat is active and slightly chaotic. Her parents have stopped asking when she's settling down.

### Her values

Freedom, lightness, curiosity. She doesn't romanticise nomadism — she just finds staying still uncomfortable. She believes the way to know a thing is to travel to it, and the way to know yourself is to travel away.

## The Bandhani Maximalist

> *She doesn't match. She overrules.*

### A day in her life

She's a designer, a creative director, or runs her own boutique. Mornings are slow — she's never up before 9. Studio by 11, lunch at 2, fittings or shoots in the afternoon. The evening is always something — a launch, an opening, a dinner she's hosting for fourteen, a play. She gets home late and never alone.

### What she likes, what she doesn't

She likes red lipstick on Tuesdays, statement earrings before breakfast, dance music, theatre, terracotta walls, contrast blouses, friends who reply to texts within seven minutes, cake at unusual hours. She doesn't like beige, restraint, dimmer switches, parties that end before midnight, anyone who tells her to tone it down.

### Free time

She collects — sarees, art, friends. She throws parties. She goes to performance art shows and stays for the after-conversation. She has more clothes than weekends, and she's working on it.

### Her partner

Also creative, also a little flamboyant, possibly a chef or an artist. He doesn't compete with her for attention, mostly because he isn't capable of it, and that suits both of them. They host together; he cooks while she introduces people.

### Her family

Theatrical. Big personalities. Possibly a parent who was a performer, a designer, or in advertising. Family functions are loud, late, well-fed. Childhood photographs feature unusually elaborate costumes for ordinary occasions.

### Her values

Expression, joy, generosity, presence. She believes that taking up space is a service to other people — a permission slip. She doesn't dress for herself; she dresses to make rooms warmer.

## The Tussar Intellectual

> *She reads the weaver's name before the price tag.*

### A day in her life

She's a professor, a long-form journalist, a researcher at an NGO, or a curator at a museum. She's up at 6, walks for thirty minutes, writes for two hours before checking email. Class or interview by 11, lunch at her desk, more work, an evening event — book launch, panel discussion, a screening at a film club. Dinner is a dosa at a regular place, or at home with one or two friends.

### What she likes, what she doesn't

She likes weaver co-ops, regional cinema, second-hand bookshops, single malt at home, Carnatic music she doesn't fully understand, hand-block printing, the smell of new newsprint. She doesn't like fast fashion, hashtags, LinkedIn culture, billionaire interviews, the word "luxury."

### Free time

She reads — hours a day, mostly non-fiction, often history or anthropology. She supports craft co-ops, sometimes financially, always with her purchases. She hosts salon-style dinners — six people, long conversation, no music after the food arrives.

### Her partner

In a similar field. They met at a conference, or through a mutual friend who's also in academia. They argue cheerfully about books and rarely about anything that matters. He cooks; she pours.

### Her family

Educated, modest, possibly multiple postgraduate degrees. Liberal politically, restrained personally. Family dinners involve the newspaper. Her parents have signed Wikipedia entries, or might as well.

### Her values

Provenance, craft, scholarship, modesty. She believes that knowing where things come from is a form of respect — for the maker, for the material, for yourself. She finds performance exhausting and consistency dignified.

## The Ajrakh Folklorist

> *She knows the weaver's grandmother's name.*

### A day in her life

She works at a museum, runs a craft documentation project, is a senior person at an NGO, or curates exhibitions for a foundation. She's up early, drinks tea (not coffee), and spends the first hour with a book or a journal. Office or studio by 10. Her work involves travel — Kutch, Bhuj, Patan, Thanjavur, Maheshwar. Today she's home; tomorrow she's at a craft fair. Evening is often music — Sufi, classical, indie folk.

### What she likes, what she doesn't

She likes oral histories, indigo, kohl, oxidised silver stacked in threes, qawwali, the Rann at sunset, hand-stamped textiles, listening to elders, Sufi poetry in translation. She doesn't like packaging, mass production, branded heritage, or the word "ethnic."

### Free time

She collects jewellery, mostly tribal, always functional. She listens to music with intent. She supports artisans by name. She travels to craft villages and stays in homestays. She photographs hands more than faces.

### Her partner

Similar craft-conscious or artistic — a musician, a designer, a documentary photographer. They share Spotify wraps with embarrassing similarity. They've travelled together to obscure places and slept on bad mattresses cheerfully.

### Her family

Liberal, possibly artistic. A parent who was a teacher, an artist, or an activist. Supportive of unusual paths. The family home has more books than walls and at least one loom or instrument.

### Her values

Heritage, craft preservation, story, cultural memory. She believes a craft is a person, and that supporting craft means knowing the person. She finds large-scale anything suspect.

## The Mul Unrehearsed

> *She wears a saree the way other people wear jeans.*

### A day in her life

She might be a professor, a writer, a long-married woman with grown children, or simply someone unusually centred for her age. She wakes early — habit, not virtue — and walks before the city does. She wears a saree most days. Today she's in errands, then writing, then a long lunch with a friend, then the kind of afternoon that doesn't have a plan. Dinner is at home. Bed by 10.

### What she likes, what she doesn't

She likes hot tea, broken-in shoes, the same five sarees on rotation, simple food, books, long walks, conversations with strangers, the smell of rain on hot stone. She doesn't like fuss, performance, "occasion-wear" as a category, ironing, photographs of herself.

### Free time

She walks. She cooks, often something nobody's asked for. She gardens or reads or watches old films. Her hobbies look like life and her life looks like hobbies. She doesn't draw a line.

### Her partner

Long-married or settled. Equally low-fuss. They don't do dates — they do walks. He thinks she's beautiful in a way that doesn't require her to perform, which is a relief. They've had the same arguments enough times to have stopped having them.

### Her family

Comfortable, mid-sized. Sunday lunch at someone's house. Children, if she has them, are people she actually knows. The household is the kind where the house feels lived-in, not styled.

### Her values

Ease, presence, integrity. She believes that the most important thing about a saree is that you wear it, and the second most important thing is that you forget you're wearing it. She suspects most adults are still trying to be liked. She isn't.

## The Banarasi Occasionalist

> *She has three sarees. She knows when each one comes out.*

### A day in her life

She works in tech, finance, consulting, or runs her own company. Most days she's in jeans or trousers. Today, however, is the day before Diwali. She's at the tailor's in the morning getting a fall stitched. Lunch is brief and at her desk. Afternoon she texts a friend asking jhumkas or studs. Evening she remembers she hasn't oiled her hair in two weeks. By 10 the next morning, she's draped, photographed, and at her in-laws'.

### What she likes, what she doesn't

She likes weddings, Diwali, planning outfits a week in advance, group chats about what to wear, getting professionally draped, finding a saree she can wear to multiple events. She doesn't like surprise dress codes, draping solo, the assumption that she'll wear ethnic-wear daily, last-minute blouse alterations.

### Free time

Saree-adjacent: nothing, mostly. Otherwise: gym, weekend cocktails, work-related socialising, travel that involves other clothes entirely. She has hobbies; sarees aren't one of them.

### Her partner

Similarly professional, similarly trousers-default. He's helpful with the back-pleat-pin moment and uncomplaining about it. He thinks she looks great when she's dressed up and tells her so, which she registers and then denies.

### Her family

Modern, urban, festival-observant but not ritualistic. Diwali matters. Karva Chauth is a debate. Her mother sometimes calls to ask why she doesn't wear sarees more — not pushy, just wistful.

### Her values

Practicality, planning, polish. She believes that things should fit into a life, and that a life with weddings and Diwali doesn't have room for daily saree management. She also believes — quietly, occasionally — that she should wear them more. She'll get there.

## The Linen Modernist

> *A saree on a Tuesday. Quietly.*

### A day in her life

She's an architect, a product designer, a strategy consultant, or runs an interiors studio. She's up at 7, espresso, twenty minutes of email triage, in office by 9.30. Today she's wearing a linen saree to a client meeting — a structured blouse, a brogue, a silver pendant. The room notices but doesn't say. Lunch is with a colleague at a café she chose. The afternoon is a design review. Evening is a glass of wine and a kindle.

### What she likes, what she doesn't

She likes clean architecture, single-origin coffee, well-edited spaces, structured silhouettes, capsule wardrobes, modernist literature, Anavila, Raw Mango, brutalist buildings, restaurants with five things on the menu. She doesn't like clutter, decorative-anything, theatrical traditions, group jewellery shopping.

### Free time

Design pursuits — pottery, perhaps, or print-making, or a long-running interest in industrial design history. Long runs. Reading. She treats hobbies the way she treats projects: limited number, well chosen, gone deep.

### Her partner

Similarly design-adjacent or analytical. They live together with admirable lack of mess. They cook on weekends, read in parallel on the same sofa, travel well. Neither of them posts about the other on Instagram.

### Her family

Small, settled, often nuclear. A parent might be in design, architecture, or academia. They text more than they call.

### Her values

Clarity, discipline, function. She believes a saree can be workwear — should be — and that the only thing standing between modern Indian women and the saree is bad styling, bad framing, and too many rules. She's quietly building a different one.

## How to use these

When writing card copy, picture the person before you write the line. The tagline should sound like something she would claim on X. The description should sound like something a friend who knows her would say about her, not something a brand would say.

When writing quiz questions, the test of a good question is whether her answer would be predictable to anyone who knows her well. If two archetypes would answer the same way, the question isn't doing work. Keep cutting until each option clearly belongs to one or two archetypes.

When writing brand voice, lean into specificity. Generic warmth is the enemy. The Saree DNA voice should feel observational — slightly dry, occasionally funny, never aspirational. The archetypes should never sound aspirational either; they should sound true.

A final note. These profiles will date. Update them yearly. The Linen Modernist of 2026 is not the Linen Modernist of 2030. Living archetypes outperform static ones.
